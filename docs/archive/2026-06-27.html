🧠 Data Science Topic of the Day

🎓 SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)

The gold standard for explaining why your model said what it said — one prediction at a time.

What It Is

SHAP is a model-agnostic explainability framework rooted in cooperative game theory. It assigns each input feature a 'Shapley value' — a fair share of credit (or blame) for a model's output. Unlike older methods such as feature importance scores or LIME, SHAP values are mathematically consistent: they satisfy axioms of fairness (efficiency, symmetry, dummy, additivity) borrowed from economics. The result is that for any single prediction, you can decompose the model's output into a signed contribution from every feature, all of which sum exactly to the difference between that prediction and the model's average prediction. This makes SHAP the closest thing machine learning has to a 'ground truth' explanation layer.

How It Works

1. BASELINE: Establish E[f(x)], the model's mean prediction across the training set. This is the starting point for every explanation. 2. COALITION SAMPLING: For a given prediction, SHAP considers all possible subsets ('coalitions') of features. For each coalition, it measures the model's output with and without the feature of interest — marginalising out the missing features using background data. 3. SHAPLEY VALUE: The feature's contribution is the weighted average marginal contribution across all coalitions. This is the classic Shapley formula from 1953 game theory. 4. EFFICIENT ALGORITHMS: Exact computation is exponential in the number of features. In practice: TreeSHAP (exact, fast for XGBoost/Random Forests/LightGBM using tree structure), KernelSHAP (model-agnostic, approximate, slower), DeepSHAP (neural nets via backprop), and LinearSHAP (closed-form for linear models). 5. VISUALISATION LAYERS: • Waterfall plot: single prediction breakdown, feature by feature. • Beeswarm / summary plot: global feature importance + direction across the dataset. • Dependence plot: how one feature's SHAP value varies with its raw value (interaction effects visible). • Force plot: compact visual of a single prediction showing pushes toward and away from the baseline.

When To Use

• Any time a model output must be justified to a human stakeholder (credit decisions, sales scoring, risk flags). • Regulated or high-stakes contexts where 'the model said so' is not an acceptable answer (GDPR right-to-explanation, fair lending). • Debugging model behaviour — SHAP is often the fastest way to spot data leakage or unexpected feature reliance. • Feature selection: Shapley-based importance is far more reliable than default impurity-based importance in tree models. • A/B test readouts where you want to understand *why* a variant moved a metric, not just *that* it moved. • Do NOT lean on SHAP as your sole explainability tool when features are highly correlated — Shapley values distribute credit across correlated features in ways that can look small and misleading; pair with partial dependence plots in that scenario.

🎯 Worked Example — D&B Context

D&B trains an XGBoost propensity-to-buy model (Buydex-adjacent) predicting which SMB accounts are likely to purchase a Finance & Risk data subscription in the next 90 days. The model ingests ~80 features: DUNS-linked firmographic signals (employee count, industry, corporate family tree depth), Paydex trajectory (3-month delta), technographic stack, past D&B product usage, and derived temporal signals like 'days since last contract renewal.' A rep flags an anomaly: a mid-market logistics firm scored 92/100 but the AE says the account went cold six months ago. You run TreeSHAP on that single prediction: • Paydex_delta_90d: +18.4 pts (firm's payment behaviour improved sharply — model treats this as buy-readiness signal) • corporate_family_subsidiaries: +12.1 pts (recently added 4 subsidiaries — model sees compliance expansion need) • days_since_renewal: +9.7 pts (contract window opening) • last_login_recency: −11.2 pts (no product logins in 60 days — model partially discounts) • technographic_erp_sap: +7.3 pts The waterfall plot makes the story legible in 30 seconds: the high score is driven almost entirely by structural corporate change signals and a payment behaviour uptick — not engagement signals, which are actually dragging the score down. The AE updates the outreach angle: instead of a generic renewal call, they lead with 'we noticed you added four subsidiaries — your current coverage may have gaps.' This is D&B's Explainability First / Ground Truth principle in action: SHAP turns a black-box propensity score into a specific, actionable reason the rep can use in the first sentence of the email.
