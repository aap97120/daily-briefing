🧠 Data Science Topic of the Day

🎓 Survival Analysis (Time-to-Event Modeling)

Stop asking 'will it happen?' — start asking 'when will it happen, and what's slowing it down?'

What It Is

Survival analysis is a family of statistical techniques originally developed in clinical trials to model the time until a specific event occurs — a patient dies, a machine fails, a customer churns. In B2B analytics, the 'event' can be anything with a clock attached: a prospect converts, a contract lapses, a payment goes delinquent, an account expands. The killer feature is how it handles 'censored' observations — entities where the event hasn't happened yet by the time you look at the data. Classical regression throws those rows away or mis-codes them; survival analysis uses them correctly, because the fact that a customer has NOT churned after 18 months is itself informative signal. Core models range from the non-parametric Kaplan-Meier estimator (pure descriptive curve) to the semi-parametric Cox Proportional Hazards model (adds covariates) to fully parametric models (Weibull, log-normal) and modern extensions like DeepSurv (a neural net Cox model) and survival forests.

How It Works

1. DEFINE THE CLOCK & EVENT: Choose a time origin (contract start, first contact, onboarding date) and a single, unambiguous event (first missed payment, renewal lapse, upsell close). Every entity either experiences the event or is 'right-censored' — still alive at observation cutoff. 2. KAPLAN-MEIER CURVES: Non-parametric estimate of the survival function S(t) = P(T > t). Produces the classic stepwise curve. No covariates; used for visual EDA and segment comparisons (log-rank test tells you if two curves are statistically different). 3. COX PROPORTIONAL HAZARDS: Models the hazard rate h(t) — the instantaneous risk of the event at time t given survival to t — as h(t) = h₀(t) × exp(β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + …). The baseline hazard h₀(t) is left unspecified (semi-parametric). Coefficients are interpreted as hazard ratios: a HR of 1.4 on 'payment_days_late' means a 40% higher instantaneous churn risk per unit increase. The proportional hazards assumption (covariate effects are constant over time) must be tested — Schoenfeld residuals are the standard check. 4. PARAMETRIC MODELS: Weibull is most common — assumes hazard is monotonically increasing or decreasing over time. Useful when you want to extrapolate beyond observed data. 5. SURVIVAL FORESTS & DEEPSURV: Random Survival Forests extend random forests to censored outcomes (each tree predicts a cumulative hazard function). DeepSurv replaces the linear Cox predictor with a deep neural network — handles non-linear interactions and high-dimensional inputs. Both produce individual-level survival curves, not just average effects. 6. OUTPUT INTERPRETATION: For each entity you get S(t) — the probability of surviving (not churning, not defaulting) past time t. You can extract median survival time, restricted mean survival time (RMST), or the predicted probability of event within a specific window (e.g., next 90 days).

When To Use

USE IT WHEN: (a) Time matters — you care not just about whether something happens but when. (b) You have censored data — many observations haven't experienced the event yet. (c) You want to rank accounts by urgency, not just probability. (d) You need to model competing risks — a deal can be lost OR won OR go dark, and these compete. AVOID IT WHEN: You only have a snapshot with no time dimension, or your event horizon is fixed and all outcomes are observed (plain logistic regression is simpler). SWEET SPOTS FOR D&B: Payment delinquency timing (Paydex trajectories predicting days-to-default), contract renewal risk windows, prospect-to-close velocity by firmographic segment, and 'buying window' detection — the temporal analytics pillar of D&B's AI Decision Intelligence strategy maps directly onto survival analysis logic.

They want to know not just 'who will default?' but 'who is likely to default in the next 60 days?' so relationship managers can intervene proactively. MODEL BUILD: Time origin = loan origination date. Event = first payment 90+ days overdue. Censored = current borrowers with no delinquency yet.
