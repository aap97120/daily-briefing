🧠 Data Science Topic of the Day

🎓 Survival Analysis

Predicting not just IF something happens — but WHEN.

What It Is

Survival analysis is a family of statistical methods originally developed in medical research to model time-to-event data — how long until a patient dies, relapses, or recovers. In a B2B analytics context, the 'event' can be anything with a clock attached: customer churn, contract renewal, late payment, company default, or a prospect converting to a sale. The defining feature is that survival models handle 'censored' observations gracefully — meaning they can learn from records where the event simply hasn't happened yet (a customer who is still active, a loan that hasn't defaulted). Ordinary regression and classification models either discard those records or treat them incorrectly. Survival analysis treats partial information as signal, not noise. Core models include the Kaplan-Meier estimator (non-parametric curves), the Cox Proportional Hazards model (semi-parametric, the workhorse), and increasingly, deep learning variants like DeepHit and the survival-adapted transformer.

How It Works

Three concepts anchor everything. (1) The Survival Function S(t): the probability that the event has NOT yet occurred by time t. At t=0, S(0)=1; it decays monotonically toward 0. (2) The Hazard Function h(t): the instantaneous rate at which the event occurs at time t, given survival up to t — think of it as 'danger per unit of time.' (3) Censoring: a record is right-censored when we stop observing it before the event occurs (contract still live at data extract date). The Cox Proportional Hazards model avoids assuming any specific shape for the baseline hazard h₀(t) — instead it models how covariates (features) multiplicatively shift that baseline: h(t|X) = h₀(t) × exp(β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + ... + βₚXₚ). The exp(β) coefficients are hazard ratios — a hazard ratio of 1.4 for 'Days Since Last Purchase > 90' means that segment has 40% higher churn hazard at any point in time. Model output for a given entity is a survival curve — a personalised probability-over-time trajectory — not just a single score. This makes it ideal for ranking accounts by urgency: who is likely to churn in the next 30 days vs. 90 days vs. 180 days.

When To Use

Use survival analysis whenever time-to-event matters more than a binary yes/no classification. Key signals: your training data contains a mix of completed events and still-active records (censoring); you need to prioritise by urgency, not just likelihood; you want to compare risk across segments over a time horizon. Ideal D&B use cases: (1) Churn / Non-Renewal Prediction — score every customer account with a 30/60/90-day survival curve. (2) Payment Default Timing — Paydex trajectory feeds directly into hazard features; predict not just 'will they default' but 'in how many days.' (3) Prospect Conversion Windows — model time-to-first-purchase to identify which prospects are in an active buying window right now (D&B's Buydex intent signal maps directly to a hazard spike). (4) Company Distress / Cessation — corporate family tree + financial stress indicators → survival curve for business failure. Avoid it when you have no meaningful time dimension, very few events, or when all records are fully observed with no censoring.

🎯 Worked Example — D&B Context

Scenario: D&B's Account Intelligence Agent needs to surface which of 8,000 mid-market customers in a sales rep's book are most likely to churn before contract renewal — with enough lead time to intervene. A Cox model is trained on three years of contract history. Features include: Paydex score trend (90-day delta), number of D&B data API calls in last 30 days (product engagement), days since last executive sponsor contact, corporate family tree changes (M&A activity detected via DUNS linkage), and industry distress index. The model outputs a personalised survival curve for each account. Account A — a logistics firm — shows S(60 days) = 0.91 (low urgency). Account B — a mid-size retailer — shows S(60 days) = 0.54, meaning there is a 46% probability of churn within 60 days. The hazard ratio analysis flags that Account B's Paydex score dropped 18 points over 90 days (hazard ratio 1.6x) and API call volume fell 70% (hazard ratio 2.1x) — both are dominant churn drivers. The Agentforce integration surfaces Account B at the top of the rep's queue with a plain-English explainer: 'Payment behaviour deteriorating + product disengagement = high 60-day churn risk.' The rep books a call.
