🧠 Data Science Topic of the Day

🎓 Knowledge Graphs

Structured relationship intelligence — turning isolated facts into connected, queryable business reality

What It Is

A knowledge graph is a network of real-world entities (companies, people, products, events) and the typed relationships between them, stored in a graph structure where nodes represent entities and edges represent relationships with semantic meaning. Unlike a relational database where relationships are implied through foreign keys and joins, a knowledge graph makes relationships first-class citizens — they carry attributes, directionality, and type. The underlying data model is typically a triple: (Subject → Predicate → Object), e.g. (Acme Corp → subsidiary_of → GlobalHoldings Inc). This triple-store approach, often represented in RDF or property graph formats (Neo4j, Amazon Neptune, TigerGraph), allows machines to reason over connected data at scale. Knowledge graphs power Google's Knowledge Panel, LinkedIn's economic graph, and critically — D&B's own corporate family tree and entity resolution network, which is itself one of the world's largest commercial knowledge graphs.

How It Works

1. ENTITY EXTRACTION & RESOLUTION: Raw data sources (filings, web, firmographics, news) are parsed to extract entities. Crucially, the same real-world entity appearing under different names ('IBM,' 'International Business Machines,' 'IBM Corp') must be resolved to a single canonical node — this is exactly what DUNS numbers do. Without this step, the graph is full of duplicates and broken links. 2. RELATIONSHIP TYPING: Each edge is given a semantic type (owns, competes_with, supplies_to, employs, board_member_of). Typed edges allow graph queries to be meaningful rather than just topological. 3. ONTOLOGY / SCHEMA LAYER: A formal ontology defines what entity types exist, what relationship types are valid between them, and what constraints apply (a Person cannot be a subsidiary_of a Company). This schema is the 'rules of the world' the graph enforces. 4. GRAPH STORAGE & INDEXING: Property graphs (Neo4j) store attributes directly on nodes and edges and support the Cypher query language. RDF triple-stores (Apache Jena, Stardog) use SPARQL. Both support traversal queries like 'find all companies within 3 hops of this bankrupt entity' efficiently via adjacency list indexing. 5. GRAPH TRAVERSAL & REASONING: Queries walk the graph hop by hop. A 2-hop traversal from a target company surfaces its subsidiaries' subsidiaries. A path query finds the shortest ownership chain between two entities. Reasoning engines can infer implicit relationships (if A owns B and B owns C, then A transitively controls C). 6. GRAPH EMBEDDINGS (LINK PREDICTION): Algorithms like TransE, RotatE, or Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) learn vector representations of nodes and edges in continuous space. The geometric relationship between vectors encodes the semantic relationship type. This enables link prediction — 'what relationship is most likely missing from this graph?' — which is powerful for discovering undisclosed corporate relationships or likely supplier connections. 7. INTEGRATION WITH LLMs: Modern pipelines combine knowledge graphs with LLMs via GraphRAG — instead of retrieving flat document chunks, the retrieval step pulls structured subgraphs around relevant entities and feeds them as structured context into the prompt. This dramatically improves factual grounding and relationship-aware reasoning.

When To Use

Use knowledge graphs when: (a) your problem is fundamentally relational — the VALUE is in connections, not just attributes; (b) you need multi-hop reasoning ('who are the ultimate beneficial owners of this counterparty, and do any of them appear on a sanctions list?'); (c) entity disambiguation is critical and the same real-world thing appears under many names across data sources; (d) you need explainable, auditable reasoning paths rather than black-box scores; (e) your domain has a rich, well-defined ontology (corporate structure, supply chains, financial networks). Do NOT default to a knowledge graph when your data is genuinely tabular with no meaningful relational structure, or when you need simple aggregation — a data warehouse is faster and simpler for that. Knowledge graphs shine in risk propagation, fraud network detection, supply chain mapping, org hierarchy navigation, and competitive intelligence.

🎯 Worked Example — D&B Context

SCENARIO — Pipeline Risk & Revenue Protection Agent at D&B: A D&B enterprise customer (a Fortune 500 bank) uses an Account Intelligence agent to monitor credit exposure across its commercial loan portfolio. The challenge: a borrower 'Meridian Logistics LLC' shows healthy Paydex scores, but the bank's exposure is actually concentrated across 11 related entities it doesn't know are connected. KNOWLEDGE GRAPH IN ACTION: — D&B's corporate family tree knowledge graph contains Meridian Logistics LLC as a node, connected via 'subsidiary_of' edges up through two holding companies to a single ultimate beneficial owner: 'Apex Capital Partners.' — Apex Capital Partners has a 'board_member_of' edge to a director who also sits on the board of 'Cascade Freight Inc,' a separate borrower at the same bank. — A 3-hop traversal query surfaces that 4 of Cascade Freight's top 5 suppliers are also Meridian subsidiaries — meaning a Meridian failure creates a cascade supplier shock to Cascade Freight simultaneously. — The graph also links Apex Capital Partners to a 'legal_action' node (an SEC filing ingested 6 days ago), which wasn't in any flat data feed the bank monitored. OUTPUT: The Pipeline Risk Agent surfaces a single alert: 'Your apparent $12M exposure to Meridian Logistics is economically $47M when all connected entities are resolved via corporate family tree. An SEC enforcement action against the ultimate parent was filed 6 days ago. Recommend immediate credit review.' This is impossible with a flat database query. It requires entity resolution (DUNS as the canonical node ID), typed relationships (subsidiary_of, board_member_of, supplies_to), multi-hop traversal (3 hops to find the exposure concentration), and real-time event node attachment (SEC filing). Every component is a D&B structural moat capability.
